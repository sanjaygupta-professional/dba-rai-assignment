The business problem

Reviewers spend their time assembling evidence, not deciding

A mid-market payer adjudicates ~650K prior-authorizations/yr. The costly, slow, clinically sensitive segment is the human-reviewed tail — where a licensed UM nurse must touch the case.
~390K
cases/yr reach a human reviewer (≈60% of volume)
35–45%
of touch-time is assembling & reconciling — not judgment
~$4.1M
annual reviewer labor on that segment (provisional)

So-what: today the nurse pulls notes, finds the applicable policy version, and checks criteria by hand before deciding. We assert — and must prove — that pre-assembling that packet with AI converts assembly time into decision time.

02
Ideation summary

AI prepares — the human owns the adverse decision

The value we test is AI (RAG-grounded reasoning over unstructured notes vs. medical-necessity criteria) — not Automation (the deterministic eligibility/code/benefit checks stay RPA, out of scope) and not Analytics.

What the reviewer receives

Not a "score" — a decision packet: evidence + the policy checklist with each criterion marked met / unmet / uncertain, cited to source, plus a recommendation: approve · pend-for-info · route-for-review.

Hard constraint (RAI)

The AI's most adverse possible action is route-for-review or pend. It cannot deny. Every denial is owned by a licensed clinician. Augmentation, never automation of the adverse call.

Scope for the test: the human-reviewed segment, two service lines — advanced imaging + specialty pharmacy (high volume, stable published criteria).

03
The one hypothesis we test

One powered value endpoint. Safety as guardrails.

≥ 35%
reduction in median reviewer touch-time per case vs. no-packet control. The only number that converts to money.

Guardrail 1 — hard stop (powered)

Packet groundedness ≥ 95%, measured per assertion (thousands). A groundedness failure vetoes the build regardless of the speed gain — speed bought with fabrication is not a trade we may make.

Guardrail 2 — directional flag

Overturn proxy ≤ 5%. At N=500 a ~5% event can't be statistically confirmed; a rise is a red flag that triggers a powered safety study, not an in-test kill. Honest about what the cheap test can't settle.

04
Data document · summary

Clean structure, messy where it matters

~75–80% of the decision-relevant content is unstructured — free-text notes + a version-drifting policy corpus. That is exactly where the AI earns its keep, and where quality risk concentrates.

Sources

  • Real test: payer's ~500 historical adjudicated PA cases (PHI, on-prem, HIPAA limited-data-set).
  • Public proxies (PHI-free harness): MIMIC-IV notes, CMS NCD/LCD criteria, ICD-10/HCPCS/CPT, Synthea (synthetic FHIR), X12 278 samples.

Cleanliness rating: 3 / 5

  • Structured (codes, labs, claims): ~4–5, near-complete.
  • Clinical notes: ~2–3 — abbreviation-heavy, OCR-contaminated where faxed.
  • Policy corpus: ~3 — version drift, picking the applicable edition is itself error-prone.

Rating set by what could fail silently, not the average field.

05
Data rigor · the four checks

Where data quietly breaks a model

Missing data

Impute only below the 15–20% ceiling; above it, imputation fabricates. Missing clinical evidence is signal (→ "uncertain / PEND"), never imputed. Structured fields over the ceiling → drop the case.

Data leakage — the sharpest risk

We score against historical outcomes, so the label must never enter packet assembly. The AI sees only evidence available at decision time; outcome revealed only at scoring. (The elevator-maintenance trap.)

Training / historical bias

Danger isn't invented bias — it's learning historical denial bias as truth. Label = efficiency benchmark only, never fairness. Stratify by age×sex×payer-line as a directional flag → powered fairness sub-study.

Recency / relevance

Policy pinned to the edition effective at each case's decision date; code sets pinned to case-year; recent case window. More data isn't better — relevant, current data is.

These four are the bars raised in the 11-July-prep session — answered explicitly in the standalone data document.

07
Why this is a prototype — not a product

Built to be graded, then thrown away

It deliberately is NOT

  • No live PA, no EHR integration, no UI
  • Automates no decision
  • Ignores latency, scaling, MLOps, FHIR plumbing
  • Thrown away win or lose

It validates the BUSINESS case

  • Does pre-assembly buy reviewer time safely?
  • Retrospective cases with known answers → gradeable
  • Tests 1 falsifiable hypothesis, not a feature set
  • Fails fast & cheap: 6 reviewers, 4 weeks, $35K

$35K test gates a ~$1.6M build decision — under 2.5% of its cost. That asymmetry is the whole point.

Responsible-AI surface · DARWIN-R

The four questions a leader must be able to answer

Bias / fairness

Don't reward historical denial bias as "accuracy." Stratified reporting → powered fairness sub-study. Label ≠ fairness benchmark.

Privacy (HIPAA)

PHI never leaves the on-prem boundary; only de-identified criterion summaries reach the managed LLM. No name/MRN in any prompt.

Explainability

Every assertion carries a source citation + met/unmet status. The packet is the explanation — traceable in seconds.

Human authority

AI's most adverse action is route/pend. Every denial owned by a licensed clinician. Guardrails keep augmentation from becoming unsafe automation.

The go / no-go

  • PROCEED only if the value band clears cost — flags route to a powered follow-up
